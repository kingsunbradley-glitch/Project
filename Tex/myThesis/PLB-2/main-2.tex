\title{Isomeric States in $^{269}$Hs and Shell Structure near $N=162$ Probed through the Decay of $^{273}$Ds}

\author{Z. R. Sun}
\affiliation{State Key Laboratory of Heavy Ion Science and Technology, Institute of Modern Physics, Chinese Academy of Sciences, Lanzhou 730000, China}
\affiliation{School of Nuclear Science and Technology, University of Chinese Academy of Sciences, Beijing 100049, China}

\begin{abstract}
A series of experiments was carried out at the SHANS-2 separator at IMP CAS. The cross section of the $^{238}$U($^{40}$Ar, $5n$)$^{273}$Ds reaction was investigated over the excitation-energy range of 42--50 MeV. The same reaction had previously been studied in a JINR--IMP CAS collaborative experiment at the DGFRS-2 separator at a comparable excitation energy of 49 MeV. In that study, two decay chains of $^{273}$Ds were observed with different $\alpha$-decay energies and lifetimes, which suggested the coexistence of a ground state and an isomeric state. In the present work, excitation energies of 42, 45, and 50 MeV were examined in detail. Upper limits of 0.34 and 1.1 pb were obtained for the production cross section at 42 and 45 MeV, respectively, whereas two decay chains were observed at 50 MeV, corresponding to a cross section of $0.14^{+0.18}_{-0.08}$ pb. This value is consistent with the $0.18^{+0.44}_{-0.12}$ pb reported from DGFRS-2. Notably, one chain attributed to the isomeric state at SHANS-2 follows a previously unreported decay path. In addition, the possible existence of an isomeric state in $^{269}$Hs is discussed on the basis of the $\alpha$-particle energy distribution of $^{269}$Hs populated through different production routes.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{introduction}

The macroscopic liquid-drop model does not predict the existence of superheavy elements (SHEs, $Z \ge 104$), because Coulomb repulsion dominates in this region. Their experimental synthesis therefore reflects the stabilizing role of quantum shell effects. Major experimental and theoretical advances, especially in $^{48}\mathrm{Ca}$-induced reactions, have made it possible to produce nuclei in this extreme mass region \cite{oganessianSuperheavyNuclei48Cainduced2015, oganessianInvestigationCa482022}. These shell effects lead to the predicted islands of stability in the superheavy region. Enhanced stability is expected near the deformed doubly magic shell closures at $Z = 108$ and $N = 162$ \cite{dvorakDoublyMagicNucleus2006}, and toward heavier spherical configurations near $N = 184$ and $Z = 114\text{--}126$, although the exact location depends on the theoretical model \cite{kruppa2000ShellCorrectionsSuperheavy}. Odd-$A$ nuclei in this region provide stringent tests of these models because their decay properties offer direct information on the underlying single-particle structure around the deformed $N = 162$ shell gap. In this context, the odd-neutron nucleus $^{273}\mathrm{Ds}$ can be regarded as a one-neutron system above the $N = 162$ gap, whereas its $\alpha$-decay daughter $^{269}\mathrm{Hs}$ represents a one-neutron-hole system below this gap. The $\alpha$-decay properties connecting $^{273}\mathrm{Ds}$ and $^{269}\mathrm{Hs}$ are therefore highly sensitive to the shell structure near $N = 162$.

The isotope $^{273}\mathrm{Ds}$ was first identified unambiguously at the GSI velocity filter SHIP as an $\alpha$-decay product in cold-fusion reactions \cite{hofmann2002NewResultsElements}. In the $^{208}\mathrm{Pb}(^{70}\mathrm{Zn}, n)^{277}\mathrm{Cn}$ experiments performed at GSI \cite{hofmann2002NewResultsElements,hofmann1996NewElement112} and RIKEN \cite{sumita2013NewResultProduction,morita2007ExperimentSynthesisIsotope277}, the measured lifetime of $^{273}\mathrm{Ds}$ was consistently of the order of $0.1\ \mathrm{ms}$. More recently, Oganessian et al.\ \cite{oganessian2024SynthesisDecayProperties} investigated the direct production of $^{273}\mathrm{Ds}$ in the $^{238}\mathrm{U}(^{40}\mathrm{Ar}, 5n)$ reaction. Two genetically correlated decay chains were reported \cite{geneticcorrelations}; one agreed with the known decay pattern, whereas the other was attributed to an isomeric state of $^{273}\mathrm{Ds}$. Because both chains were incomplete, however, the available information on the decay of the daughter nucleus $^{269}\mathrm{Hs}$ remained limited. Wang et al.\ \cite{Wang2024} subsequently analyzed these sequences with the density-dependent cluster model (DDCM+) and the unified decay formula (UDF), and proposed minor revisions to the decay assignments.

In the present work, the same reaction was studied at the SHANS-2 facility \cite{xu2023GasfilledRecoilSeparator}. Two additional complete decay chains were observed and assigned through established genetic correlations \cite{geneticcorrelations}. Together with the historical data, these chains provide independent support for the coexistence of two states in $^{273}\mathrm{Ds}$, denoted here as $^{273}\mathrm{Ds}^{a}$ and $^{273}\mathrm{Ds}^{b}$ following Ref.~\cite{oganessian2024SynthesisDecayProperties}. On this basis, the present study examines the possible existence of an isomeric state in $^{273}\mathrm{Ds}$ and explores whether a related isomeric structure may also exist in $^{269}\mathrm{Hs}$. By comparing the observed decay patterns with macroscopic-microscopic considerations, we further discuss the implications of these results for the neutron shell structure near $N = 162$.

\section{Experiment}

The experiment was carried out at the China Accelerator Facility for Superheavy Elements (CAFE2) at IMP, using the gas-filled recoil separator SHANS-2 (Spectrometer for Heavy Atoms and Nuclear Structure-2). The nucleus $^{273}\text{Ds}$ was produced through the $^{238}\text{U}(^{40}\text{Ar}, 5n)$ fusion-evaporation reaction. A $^{40}\text{Ar}^{13+}$ beam with average incident energies of 217, 220, and 225 MeV was delivered by the superconducting linear accelerator. The typical beam intensity on target was about $5\text{ p}\mu\text{A}$, and the total effective irradiation time was approximately 728 h.

The targets were prepared by magnetron sputtering of $^{238}\text{U}$ onto Ti backings with thicknesses of 2.22 and 2.19 $\mu\text{g/cm}^2$, giving average $^{238}\text{U}$ layer thicknesses of 470 and 604 $\mu\text{g/cm}^2$, respectively. The arc-shaped target segments were mounted on a wheel of 50 cm diameter and rotated at 1500 rpm during irradiation in order to withstand the intense beam load.

Evaporation residues (ERs) were separated in flight from the primary beam and other reaction products by SHANS-2. The separator was filled with helium at a pressure of about 1 mbar, and the magnetic rigidity was optimized for the transmission of $^{273}\text{Ds}$. At the focal plane, the ERs passed through two multi-wire proportional chambers (MWPCs) before being implanted into a double-sided silicon strip detector (DSSD). The MWPCs were located about 20 cm upstream of the DSSD and were operated with isobutane gas. They were used to distinguish implantation events from decay events by combining time-of-flight (TOF) information with MWPC trigger signals.

The DSSD has an active area of $128 \times 48\text{ mm}^2$ and a thickness of 300 $\mu\text{m}$. The front side, defined as the Y plane, is segmented into 48 horizontal strips, whereas the back side, defined as the X plane, is segmented into 128 vertical strips. This arrangement forms 6144 pixels and provides a position resolution of about 1 mm. To detect $\alpha$ particles and spontaneous-fission (SF) fragments escaping from the front side of the DSSD, six single-sided silicon detectors (SSDs) were installed upstream of the DSSD in a box configuration. Each SSD has a thickness of 500 $\mu\text{m}$ and an active area of $120 \times 63\text{ mm}^2$. In addition, three silicon detectors with thicknesses of 300 $\mu\text{m}$ were mounted immediately behind the DSSD as veto detectors to reject light particles punching through the focal-plane detectors. The entire silicon detector system was cooled to approximately $-30^\circ\text{C}$ with a circulating liquid-alcohol system in order to suppress thermal noise and improve the energy resolution. For full-energy $\alpha$ particles in the 5--9 MeV range, the typical energy resolution of the DSSD was about 30 keV (FWHM). The detection efficiency for $\alpha$ particles was 87(6)\%. All detector signals were processed with 14-bit analog-to-digital converters operating at 100 MHz in triggerless mode after preamplification and shaping. Further details of SHANS-2 and the detection system can be found in Refs.~\cite{xu2023GasfilledRecoilSeparator,sheng2021IonopticalDesignMultiparticle}.

In the present work, the silicon detectors were calibrated with standard $\alpha$ sources (\ce{^{239}Pu}, \ce{^{241}Am}, and \ce{^{244}Cm}) and with the well-known $\alpha$ lines of \ce{^{218}Rn} \cite{SINGH2019405}, \ce{^{214}Po} \cite{ZHU20211a}, \ce{^{216}Po} \cite{WU20071057}, and \ce{^{217}At} \cite{KONDEV2018382}. The reference energies were taken from the evaluated \textit{Nuclear Data Sheets}. The cross sections were deduced with simulations performed according to the method described in Ref.~\cite{zhang2026MonteCarloSimulation}, which gave a transmission efficiency of 20\%. The experimental conditions and the measured cross sections are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:cross_sections}.

\section{Results}

For the $^{238}\text{U}(^{40}\text{Ar}, xn)^{278-x}\text{Ds}$ reaction at an excitation energy of about 50 MeV, the $5n$ evaporation channel is expected to dominate \cite{oganessian2024SynthesisDecayProperties}. At this excitation energy, two decay chains were observed. Their full correlations are shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:decay-chain}.

\begin{figure}
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth]{decayChain.pdf}
	\caption{Two decay chains assigned to $^{273}$Ds observed in the $^{238}$U($^{40}$Ar, $5n$)$^{273}$Ds reaction. For each chain, the energy of the evaporation-residue (ER) event and the corresponding $X/Y$ implantation position are given at the top. The energies of the successive $\alpha$ decays and the terminal spontaneous-fission (SF) event, together with the time intervals between adjacent events, are listed below. Energies in parentheses indicate summed signals. Italic values denote the corresponding energy uncertainties. Red text and red frames mark the information established in the present work and the assignments related to the proposed isomeric states.}
	\label{fig:decay-chain}
\end{figure}

The energy and decay time of $\alpha_{1}$ in Chain 1 are consistent with the values reported in the cold-fusion $^{208}\text{Pb}(^{70}\text{Zn}, n)^{277}\text{Cn}$ experiments at GSI \cite{hofmann2002NewResultsElements,hofmann1996NewElement112} and RIKEN \cite{sumita2013NewResultProduction,morita2007ExperimentSynthesisIsotope277}, as well as in the hot-fusion $^{238}\text{U}(^{40}\text{Ar}, 5n)^{273}\text{Ds}$ study at JINR \cite{oganessian2024SynthesisDecayProperties}. The subsequent decay properties of $^{269}\text{Hs}$, which give rise to $\alpha_{2}$ and $\alpha_{3}$, and the terminal SF of $^{261}\text{Rf}$ in both chains are also consistent with the literature \cite{dvorakDoublyMagicNucleus2006,dullmannCm248Ne2008,dvorakObservation3Evaporation2008,graegerExperimentalStudy2382010,vonzweidorfEvidenceFormationSodium2004,habaProductionDecayProperties2011,habaProduction265Sg2012}. The decay assignments are discussed below.

D\"ullmann \textit{et al.}~\cite{dullmannCm248Ne2008} proposed that $^{261}\text{Rf}$ populated through the $\alpha$ decay of $^{265}\text{Sg}$ has two states, $^{261}\text{Rf}^{a}$ and $^{261}\text{Rf}^{b}$. The decay properties of these states were later confirmed by Haba \textit{et al.}~\cite{habaProductionDecayProperties2011} in the $^{248}\text{Cm}(^{22}\text{Ne}, 5n)^{265}\text{Sg}$ reaction. The state $^{261}\text{Rf}^{a}$ decays to $^{257}\text{No}$ by emission of an 8.28 MeV $\alpha$ particle with $T_{1/2} = 68$ s, whereas $^{261}\text{Rf}^{b}$ has $T_{1/2} = 2.6$ s and decays through an 18\% $\alpha$ branch with $E_{\alpha} = 8.51$ MeV and an 82\% SF branch \cite{habaProductionDecayProperties2011}. The terminal SF events observed in both chains of the present work are therefore assigned to $^{261}\text{Rf}^{b}$.

Dvorak \textit{et al.}~\cite{dvorakDoublyMagicNucleus2006} also suggested that the $\alpha$ decay of $^{269}\text{Hs}$ proceeds through two energies, $E_{\alpha} = 9.13$ and 8.95 MeV, feeding two states in the daughter nucleus, $^{265}\text{Sg}^{a}$ and $^{265}\text{Sg}^{b}$. These two values agree well with the observed $E_{\alpha_{2}}$ values in Chain 1 and Chain 2, respectively. The decay properties of $^{265}\text{Sg}^{a}$ and $^{265}\text{Sg}^{b}$ were later established by Haba \textit{et al.}~\cite{habaProduction265Sg2012} on the basis of high-statistics data from the $^{248}\text{Cm}(^{22}\text{Ne}, 5n)^{265}\text{Sg}$ reaction. They reported that $^{265}\text{Sg}^{a}$ with $T_{1/2} = 8.5$ s decays by 8.84 MeV $\alpha$ emission and feeds $^{261}\text{Rf}^{a}$ and $^{261}\text{Rf}^{b}$ with branching ratios of 91\% and 9\%, respectively. In contrast, $^{265}\text{Sg}^{b}$ with $T_{1/2} = 14.4$ s decays by 8.69 MeV $\alpha$ emission and feeds $^{261}\text{Rf}^{a}$ and $^{261}\text{Rf}^{b}$ with branching ratios of 20\% and 80\%, respectively. Both chains observed in the present work are thus assigned to the $^{265}\text{Sg}^{b} \rightarrow {}^{261}\text{Rf}^{b}$ sequence.

A comparison of the $E_{\alpha}$ values and decay times for nuclei in decay chains involving $^{273}$Ds is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:EalphaAndDecaytime}.

\subsection{Decay properties of $^{273}\text{Ds}$}

As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:EalphaAndDecaytime}, most data points from Chains 1 and 2 are consistent with the historical results. A clear exception concerns one chain reported in Ref.~\cite{oganessian2024SynthesisDecayProperties} with $E_{\alpha} = 10.929(14)\text{ MeV}$ and $\tau = 41.703\text{ ms}$, together with the chain observed in the present work with $E_{\alpha} = 10.858(14)\text{ MeV}$ and $\tau = 8.320\text{ ms}$. These values differ markedly from the higher-energy and shorter-lived decays measured at GSI and RIKEN. This separation in both energy and lifetime supports the existence of a long-lived state in $^{273}\text{Ds}$ in addition to the previously known state.

At present, $^{273}\text{Ds}$ has been observed through two production routes: as the $\alpha$-decay daughter of $^{277}\text{Cn}$ in the cold-fusion $^{208}\text{Pb}(^{70}\text{Zn}, n)$ reaction at GSI \cite{hofmann2002NewResultsElements,hofmann1996NewElement112} and RIKEN \cite{sumita2013NewResultProduction,morita2007ExperimentSynthesisIsotope277}, and by direct production in the hot-fusion $^{238}\text{U}(^{40}\text{Ar}, 5n)$ reaction at JINR \cite{oganessian2024SynthesisDecayProperties} and in the present SHANS-2 experiment. Two groups of $\alpha$-decay energies and lifetimes are now evident. Following Ref.~\cite{oganessian2024SynthesisDecayProperties}, the lower-energy, longer-lived state is denoted as $^{273}\text{Ds}^{a}$, whereas the higher-energy, shorter-lived state is denoted as $^{273}\text{Ds}^{b}$. An important feature is that $^{273}\text{Ds}$ populated through the decay of $^{277}\text{Cn}$ has so far been observed only in the $^{273}\text{Ds}^{b}$ state, whereas direct production populates both $^{273}\text{Ds}^{a}$ and $^{273}\text{Ds}^{b}$. This pattern further supports the interpretation of $^{273}\text{Ds}^{a}$ as a possible isomeric state.

\subsection{Decay properties of $^{269}\text{Hs}$}

T\"{u}rler \textit{et al.}~\cite{turlerNuclearStructureReaction2012} noted that the $\alpha$-decay energies of $^{269}\text{Hs}$ observed as the daughter of $^{277}\text{Cn}$ and $^{273}\text{Ds}$, 9.17--9.25 MeV, are among the highest values reported for this nuclide. They suggested two possible explanations: summing of $\alpha$-particle energy with conversion-electron energy in the focal-plane detector, or the existence of long-lived isomeric states in $^{269}\text{Hs}$ analogous to those known in $^{265}\text{Sg}$ and $^{261}\text{Rf}$. Motivated by this suggestion and by the present data, we tentatively classify the observed $^{269}\text{Hs}$ decays into three groups. Following the nomenclature adopted here, the group with $E_{\alpha} = 9.20(4)\text{ MeV}$ and $T_{1/2} = 13^{+10}_{-4}\text{ s}$ is denoted as $^{269}\text{Hs}^{a}$, and the group with $E_{\alpha} = 9.08(15)\text{ MeV}$ and $T_{1/2} = 2.8^{+13.6}_{-1.3}\text{ s}$ is denoted as $^{269}\text{Hs}^{b}$. In the present work, the decay with $E_{\alpha} = 8.915\text{ MeV}$ and $\tau = 9.363\text{ s}$ is tentatively assigned to a third state, denoted as $^{269}\text{Hs}^{c}$. Because the energy resolution of some earlier measurements was limited, $^{269}\text{Hs}^{a}$ and $^{269}\text{Hs}^{b}$ cannot always be distinguished reliably; in such cases they are grouped together as $^{269}\text{Hs}^{a+b}$.

\begin{figure}
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.35\textwidth]{distributionHs.pdf}
	\caption{Energy distributions of $\alpha$ particles assigned to $^{269}$Hs. The upper panel shows all events, the middle panel shows nuclei produced directly as evaporation residues (EVRs), and the lower panel shows nuclei populated through the $\alpha$ decay of $^{273}$Ds. The hatched bar indicates the event observed in the present work at $E_{\alpha} = 8.915$ MeV.}
	\label{fig:distributionHs}
\end{figure}

As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:distributionHs}, the directly produced EVR data extend over both the high-energy region assigned to $^{269}\text{Hs}^{a+b}$ and the lower-energy region assigned to $^{269}\text{Hs}^{c}$. Using an energy boundary of 9.0 MeV, the population ratio is $n(^{269}\text{Hs}^{a+b}):n(^{269}\text{Hs}^{c}) = 11:6$. In contrast, for $^{269}\text{Hs}$ populated through the $\alpha$ decay of $^{273}\text{Ds}$, this ratio is $n(^{269}\text{Hs}^{a+b}):n(^{269}\text{Hs}^{c}) = 6:1$. More specifically, the observed decays indicate $n(^{273}\text{Ds}^{b} \rightarrow {}^{269}\text{Hs}^{a+b}):n(^{273}\text{Ds}^{a} \rightarrow {}^{269}\text{Hs}^{c}) = 6:1$. Cross-decay branches such as $^{273}\text{Ds}^{b} \rightarrow {}^{269}\text{Hs}^{c}$ and $^{273}\text{Ds}^{a} \rightarrow {}^{269}\text{Hs}^{a+b}$ were not observed. This strong selectivity suggests that $^{269}\text{Hs}^{c}$ may correspond to an isomeric state, although further theoretical support is still required.

\section{Discussion}
%%\label{}
The present data reveal two distinct decay patterns in $^{273}$Ds and a correlated change in the population of $^{269}$Hs states. 
Together with the known multistate structure in $^{265}$Sg and $^{261}$Rf, this behavior suggests configuration-dependent $\alpha$ decay across the odd-$N$ chain around the deformed $N=162$ shell closure,
 and provides new experimental constraints on the single-particle structure in this region.
